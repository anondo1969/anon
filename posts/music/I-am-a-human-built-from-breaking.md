This spellbound light lofts a lantern where illusions can't survive;
the one who stood at the root, mankind burns alive.

And all the wayfarers, who only trail behind,
mankind swoops to snatch the one fistful they can find:

in willful freedom: lawless, self-willed, free,
the one tangled into many, as did Valmiki!*

Whom does mankind save?
　　if, just like this, into a cage
it crams what's emptier than a bird can hold:
　　a close, soft household?

I am afraid, afraid,
only afraid, afraid,
　　that I myself might kill the one…
And at least this much can be done:

I am a human built from breaking.

<div class="lyrics latin"><div class="lyrics-lang">Footnote</div>

<details>

<summary>* Valmiki</summary>

Valmiki: the Adi Kavi, the first poet, author of the Ramayana. In that epic a scattered host, the vanara legions, gathers under Rama to bring down Ravana, the tyrant of Lanka: the one swelling into the many. Yet the same Rama, bowing to the collective voice of his people, casts out his faithful wife Sita: the multitude that topples a tyrant is also the multitude that banishes the innocent one. To tangle the one into the many is therefore triumph and betrayal at once, which is the poem's own dread: that mankind, the many, is the very hand that kills the one. And the exiled Sita is given refuge in Valmiki's own hermitage, so the poet becomes keeper of the one the crowd threw away. Valmiki knew that keeping firsthand: tradition holds he began as Ratnakara, a highway robber, remade into a sage when the word mara (kill) on his lips, repeated over and over, reversed into Rama, the god's name: killing turned, syllable by syllable, into creation. That reversal sits beneath the poem's closing turn, where the fear of killing the one becomes the last line's confession: a human built from breaking.


</details>
